Pathophysiology of Acute Kidney Injury

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) is a complex syndrome characterized by a rapid decline in kidney function, leading to the accumulation of waste products and dysregulation of fluid and electrolyte balance. The pathophysiology of AKI can be broadly categorized into three main types based on the location of the insult:

1. Prerenal AKI

This is the most common form of AKI and results from inadequate renal perfusion, meaning there is insufficient blood flow to the kidneys. The kidney itself is structurally intact, but its function is impaired due to hypoperfusion.

Common causes:

  • Hypovolemia: Hemorrhage, severe dehydration, excessive fluid loss (vomiting, diarrhea, burns).
  • Decreased effective circulating volume: Congestive heart failure, liver cirrhosis with ascites, nephrotic syndrome.
  • Systemic vasodilation: Sepsis, anaphylaxis.
  • Renal vasoconstriction: NSAIDs, hepatorenal syndrome.

Note: Often reversible if corrected promptly. Prolonged severe prerenal AKI can progress to intrinsic AKI.

2. Intrinsic (or Intrarenal) AKI

This type of AKI involves direct damage to the kidney tissue itself, affecting the glomeruli, tubules, interstitium, or renal vasculature.

Most common cause: Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)

  • Ischemic ATN: Consequence of severe/prolonged prerenal AKI, leading to sustained hypoperfusion.
  • Nephrotoxic ATN: Exposure to exogenous agents (aminoglycosides, contrast dyes, cisplatin) or endogenous toxins (myoglobin from rhabdomyolysis, hemoglobin from hemolysis).

Other causes:

  • Glomerulonephritis: Inflammation of the glomeruli, often autoimmune in nature.
  • Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN): Inflammation of the renal interstitium, commonly drug-induced (e.g., penicillin, NSAIDs).
  • Vascular disorders: Conditions like vasculitis, thrombotic microangiopathies, or renal artery stenosis/embolism.

3. Postrenal AKI

This form of AKI occurs due to obstruction of urine flow anywhere from the renal pelvis to the urethra. The obstruction causes a backup of urine pressure, leading to increased pressure within Bowman's capsule, which in turn reduces the glomerular filtration rate (GFR).

  • Unilateral obstruction: If only one kidney's outflow is obstructed, the other kidney can compensate, so AKI may not be evident unless the other kidney is already impaired.
  • Bilateral obstruction: Obstruction affecting both kidneys or the outflow of a solitary kidney is necessary to cause significant AKI.

Common causes:

  • Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis): Can obstruct the ureters.
  • Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) / prostate cancer: Common causes of bladder outlet obstruction in men.
  • Tumors: Pelvic tumors compressing the ureters or bladder.
  • Blood clots: In the urinary tract.
  • Neurogenic bladder: Impaired bladder emptying due to neurological dysfunction.

Note: Often reversible if the obstruction is relieved promptly.

Disease Overview

Presentation: Rapid decline in kidney function, accumulation of waste products (e.g., creatinine, urea), fluid and electrolyte imbalances. Symptoms vary depending on severity and underlying cause, and may include oliguria/anuria, edema, nausea, fatigue, shortness of breath.

Diagnosis: Based on an abrupt decrease in kidney function, typically measured by an increase in serum creatinine or a decrease in urine output, according to established criteria (e.g., KDIGO, RIFLE).

Treatment: Management focuses on treating the underlying cause, optimizing fluid status, managing electrolyte imbalances, and avoiding further nephrotoxic insults. Renal replacement therapy (dialysis) may be required in severe cases.

Mnemonic: PRINTS (Prerenal, Intrinsic, Postrenal, Toxins, Sepsis) - Note: This mnemonic primarily covers causes rather than pathophysiology categories directly.
