Bleeding Disorders in Children

💡

Bleeding disorders in children are a significant concern, and their presentation can range from mild to life-threatening. A thorough understanding of their classification, history, examination, and laboratory investigation is crucial for timely diagnosis and management.

Classification of Bleeding Disorders ►

Bleeding disorders can be broadly classified based on the component of hemostasis that is affected:

  • Vascular Defects:
    • Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia
    • Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
    • Henoch-Schönlein purpura
    • Scurvy
  • Platelet Defects ►
    • Quantitative:
      • Immune thrombocytopenia (ITP)
      • Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP)
      • Hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS)
      • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
      • Aplastic anemia
      • Leukemia
      • Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome
      • May-Hegglin anomaly
    • Qualitative:
      • Bernard-Souvier syndrome
      • Glanzmann thrombasthenia
      • Storage pool disease
      • Drug-induced (e.g., aspirin)
  • Coagulation Factor Defects ►
    • Inherited:
      • Von Willebrand disease (vWD)
      • Hemophilia A (factor VIII deficiency)
      • Hemophilia B (factor IX deficiency)
      • Other factor deficiencies (e.g., factors VII, X, XI, XIII)
    • Acquired:
      • Vitamin K deficiency
      • Liver disease
      • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
      • Anticoagulant therapy (e.g., warfarin, heparin)
Clinical Assessment: History and Physical Examination ►
History ►

A detailed history is paramount in evaluating a child with a suspected bleeding disorder. Key areas to cover include:

  • Onset and Duration: When did the bleeding start? How long has it been occurring?
  • Type of Bleeding: Petechiae, purpura, ecchymoses, Epistaxis (nosebleeds), Gingival bleeding, Menorrhagia (heavy menstrual bleeding), Hematuria (blood in urine), Gastrointestinal bleeding, Hemarthrosis (bleeding into joints), Muscle hematomas, Post-surgical or post-traumatic bleeding.
  • Severity and Frequency: How severe is the bleeding? How often does it occur?
  • Precipitating Factors: Is there anything that triggers the bleeding? (e.g., trauma, medications)
  • Family History: History of bleeding disorders in family members, consanguinity.
  • Medication History: Use of aspirin, NSAIDs, anticoagulants, herbal remedies.
  • Past Medical History: Liver disease, kidney disease, recent infections, malignancy.
  • Neonatal History: Bleeding at circumcision, umbilical stump bleeding, intracranial hemorrhage.
Physical Examination ►

The physical examination should focus on identifying signs of bleeding and any underlying conditions:

  • General Appearance: Pallor, jaundice, signs of chronic illness.
  • Skin: Petechiae (small, pinpoint hemorrhages, non-blanching), Purpura (larger areas of hemorrhage), Ecchymoses (bruises), Telangiectasias (spider veins).
  • Mucous Membranes: Gingival bleeding, Oral petechiae.
  • Joints: Swelling, tenderness, decreased range of motion (suggestive of hemarthrosis).
  • Abdomen: Hepatosplenomegaly (may indicate liver disease or hematological malignancy).
  • Neurological Exam: Any focal neurological deficits (may indicate intracranial hemorrhage).
  • Fundoscopy: Retinal hemorrhages.
Initial Laboratory Tests ►
💡

Initial laboratory tests help to narrow down the possible causes of a bleeding disorder by assessing platelet count, coagulation pathways, and general hematological status.

The standard initial laboratory evaluation includes:

  • Complete Blood Count (CBC) with Platelet Count and Differential
    • Evaluates anemia, thrombocytopenia, and other cytopenias.
    • Can provide clues for underlying conditions like leukemia.
  • Peripheral Blood Smear
    • Assesses platelet morphology, size, and aggregation.
    • Identifies schistocytes (in DIC, TTP, HUS).
    • Detects abnormal cells (e.g., blasts in leukemia).
  • Prothrombin Time (PT)
    • Measures the extrinsic and common coagulation pathways (Factors VII, X, V, II, I).
    • Prolonged in vitamin K deficiency, liver disease, warfarin therapy, DIC, and deficiencies of these factors.
  • Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (aPTT)
    • Measures the intrinsic and common coagulation pathways (Factors XII, XI, IX, VIII, X, V, II, I).
    • Prolonged in hemophilia A and B, von Willebrand disease (sometimes), heparin therapy, DIC, and deficiencies of these factors.
  • Bleeding Time (BT)
    • Measures platelet function and vascular integrity.
    • Prolonged in platelet disorders (qualitative or severe quantitative) and severe von Willebrand disease.
    • Note: Less commonly used now due to variability and better specific tests.
  • Fibrinogen Level
    • Assesses the concentration of fibrinogen, a crucial factor in clot formation.
    • Decreased in DIC, severe liver disease, and congenital afibrinogenemia.
