Pediatric Bleeding Disorders: Mnemonic Cards

Hemophilia A
(Factor VIII Deficiency)

Presentation:
  • Neonatal/Infancy: Umbilical cord bleeding, intracranial hemorrhage (ICH), excessive bleeding after circumcision, easy bruising.
  • Toddlers/Children: Hemarthrosis (joint bleeds, especially knees, elbows, ankles), muscle hematomas, prolonged bleeding after minor trauma or dental procedures.
Diagnosis:
  • Screening Tests: Prolonged activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT), normal prothrombin time (PT), normal bleeding time, normal platelet count.
  • Confirmatory Test: Decreased Factor VIII activity.
  • Severity: Severe (< 1%), Moderate (1-5%), Mild (6-40%) Factor VIII.
Treatment:
  • Acute Bleeds: Recombinant Factor VIII replacement, desmopressin (DDAVP) for mild cases.
  • Prophylaxis: Regular infusions of Factor VIII to prevent bleeds, especially in severe cases.
  • Complications: Development of inhibitors (antibodies to Factor VIII), chronic arthropathy.

Hemophilia B
(Factor IX Deficiency)

Presentation:
  • Clinically indistinguishable from Hemophilia A; presents with similar bleeding patterns (hemarthrosis, muscle hematomas, prolonged bleeding) in infancy and childhood.
Diagnosis:
  • Screening Tests: Prolonged aPTT, normal PT, normal bleeding time, normal platelet count.
  • Confirmatory Test: Decreased Factor IX activity.
Treatment:
  • Acute Bleeds: Recombinant Factor IX replacement.
  • Prophylaxis: Regular infusions of Factor IX, especially for severe cases.

Von Willebrand Disease (VWD)

Presentation:
  • Childhood: Mucocutaneous bleeding (epistaxis, menorrhagia in adolescent girls, easy bruising, gingival bleeding), prolonged bleeding after surgery or trauma.
  • Joint bleeds are less common than in hemophilia but can occur in severe forms.
Diagnosis:
  • Initial Screening: May show prolonged bleeding time or prolonged aPTT (if Factor VIII is also low).
  • Confirmatory Tests: Decreased von Willebrand factor (VWF) antigen, decreased VWF ristocetin cofactor activity (VWF:RCo), decreased Factor VIII activity. VWF multimer analysis to classify types.
Treatment:
  • Mild Bleeds/Prophylaxis: Desmopressin (DDAVP) for Type 1 VWD.
  • Moderate/Severe Bleeds: VWF-containing Factor VIII concentrates.
  • Adjunctive: Antifibrinolytics (e.g., tranexamic acid) for mucosal bleeding.

Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)

Presentation:
  • Often follows viral infection in children.
  • Petechiae, purpura, bruising, mucosal bleeding (epistaxis, gingival bleeding).
  • Intracranial hemorrhage is rare but serious.
Diagnosis:
  • Isolated thrombocytopenia (platelet count < 100,000/µL) in an otherwise healthy child with no other obvious cause for thrombocytopenia.
  • Bone marrow examination is usually not required unless atypical features are present.
Treatment:
  • Observation: Many children with ITP recover spontaneously.
  • Corticosteroids: Prednisone or methylprednisolone.
  • Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG): Used for more severe bleeding or very low platelet counts.
  • Anti-D immunoglobulin: For Rh-positive patients.
  • Splenectomy: Rarely indicated in chronic ITP refractory to other treatments in children.

TTP vs. HUS
(Pediatric Differential)

WARNING: TTP vs. HUS in Pediatrics
While both TTP and HUS involve thrombocytopenia and microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, HUS is more common in children, often triggered by Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (STEC) infection. TTP is rarer in children.
Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS):
  • Cause: Typically associated with diarrheal illness (Shiga toxin-producing E. coli O157:H7).
  • Triad: Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, acute kidney injury (AKI).
  • Prevalence: More common in children.
Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP):
  • Cause: Deficiency of ADAMTS13 enzyme (either congenital or autoimmune).
  • Pentad (classic): Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, fever, neurological symptoms, renal impairment (less severe than HUS).
  • Prevalence: Rarer in children compared to adults.

Vitamin K Deficiency Bleeding (VKDB)

Presentation:
  • Classic VKDB (2-7 days of life): Gastrointestinal bleeding, skin bruising, bleeding from circumcision or umbilical stump.
  • Late VKDB (2 weeks to 6 months): Often more severe, commonly presents with intracranial hemorrhage (ICH), which can lead to significant morbidity and mortality.
