Bleeding Disorders in Children

IMPORTANT: Bleeding disorders are a common reason for referral in pediatrics. While often benign, they can be indicative of serious underlying conditions, necessitating a thorough and careful evaluation.

Initial Assessment

History

  • Onset, Frequency, Severity, and Site of Bleeding: Characterize the bleeding pattern.
  • Precipitating Factors: Identify any events leading to bleeding.
  • Family History: Inquire about bleeding disorders in relatives.
  • Drug History: Specifically ask about NSAIDs, antibiotics, and anticoagulants.
  • Other Medical Conditions: Assess for comorbidities.
  • Previous Procedures: Document bleeding complications from surgeries or dental work.

Examination

  • General Observations: Look for pallor, jaundice.
  • Skin: Inspect for petechiae, purpura, ecchymoses, telangiectasias.
  • Joints: Assess for signs of hemarthrosis.
  • Fundoscopy:1 Check for retinal hemorrhages.
  • Abdominal Examination: Palpate for splenomegaly or hepatomegaly.

Differential Diagnosis

Bleeding Disorders in Children
  • Common Causes:
    • Idiopathic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)
    • Von Willebrand Disease (VWD)
    • Hemophilia A and B (rare but important)
    • Vitamin K deficiency
  • Less Common Causes:
    • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)
    • Liver disease
    • Renal failure
    • Leukemia
    • Other specific factor deficiencies
    • Platelet function disorders

Key Bleeding Disorders in Pediatrics

Idiopathic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)

Presentation: Acute onset of petechiae, purpura, and bruising in an otherwise healthy child, often following a viral infection. Rarely, mucosal bleeding or intracranial hemorrhage.

Diagnosis: Diagnosis of exclusion. Platelet count < 100 x 10^9/L (often < 20 x 10^9/L) with normal peripheral blood smear. Bone marrow aspirate usually not needed unless atypical features.

Treatment: Most cases resolve spontaneously. Steroids (prednisolone) or IVIG for severe bleeding or very low platelet counts. Splenectomy is rarely needed in children.

Von Willebrand Disease (VWD)

Presentation: Mucocutaneous bleeding (epistaxis, menorrhagia, easy bruising, prolonged bleeding after trauma/surgery). Severity varies by type.

Diagnosis: Prolonged PTT (in some types), decreased Von Willebrand Factor (VWF) antigen, decreased VWF activity (ristocetin cofactor activity), decreased Factor VIII activity.

Treatment: Desmopressin (DDAVP) for mild types (Type 1). VWF-containing factor concentrates for severe types or unresponsive to DDAVP. Antifibrinolytics (tranexamic acid) for mucosal bleeding.

Hemophilia A (Factor VIII Deficiency)

Presentation: Spontaneous bleeding into joints (hemarthrosis), muscles, and soft tissues. Prolonged bleeding after trauma/surgery. Severity correlates with factor levels.

Diagnosis: Prolonged PTT, normal PT, normal bleeding time, decreased Factor VIII activity.

Treatment: Recombinant Factor VIII replacement. Prophylaxis for severe cases. Desmopressin (DDAVP) for mild hemophilia A.

Hemophilia B (Factor IX Deficiency)

Presentation: Clinically indistinguishable from Hemophilia A (spontaneous bleeding into joints, muscles, soft tissues).

Diagnosis: Prolonged PTT, normal PT, normal bleeding time, decreased Factor IX activity.

Treatment: Recombinant Factor IX replacement. Prophylaxis for severe cases.

Vitamin K Deficiency

Presentation: Neonatal bleeding (e.g., intracranial hemorrhage, gastrointestinal bleeding) due to inadequate vitamin K stores and immature liver function. Later in infancy, can be due to malabsorption or certain medications.

Diagnosis: Prolonged PT and PTT, normal bleeding time and platelet count, responds to vitamin K administration.

Treatment: Vitamin K administration (IM or IV). Prevention with prophylactic vitamin K at birth.

Investigations

  • Full Blood Count (FBC) with Peripheral Blood Film: To assess platelet count and morphology.
  • Prothrombin Time (PT): Evaluates the extrinsic and common coagulation pathways.
  • Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (APTT): Evaluates the intrinsic and common coagulation pathways.
  • Fibrinogen Level: Assesses the final step of the coagulation cascade.
  • Specific Factor Assays: For Factor VIII, Factor IX, and Von Willebrand Factor (VWF) antigen and activity, if indicated.
  • Bleeding Time: Less commonly used but can assess platelet function and vascular integrity.
  • Platelet Function Tests: If a platelet function disorder is suspected.
